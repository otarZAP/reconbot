Safety First

⚠ Batteries are not toys
Never short the 18650, charge it only on the TP4056, never leave it charging unattended, and never puncture or crush a cell.
Power off before USB
Always flip the bot's power switch OFF before plugging USB in to program it, so two power sources don't fight.
Wheels off the ground
The first time you test motors, hold the bot or prop the wheels up so it can't drive off the table.
Check + and –
Double-check plus and minus before switching on. Reversed power kills boards. If anything gets hot fast — switch off immediately.

Every wire, every pin

Wiring

1 · Power tree — build & test this first

One 18650 cell. The brain and the motors live on separate power rails on purpose: motors are electrically noisy and would otherwise reset the camera. Set the converter voltages with a multimeter before connecting any board.

Text version of the power tree

  [18650 -]─► TP4056 OUT- ─────────────┤   TPS63020 IN- ─► OUT GND  ─► ESP32-S3-CAM  GND
                                        │
                                        ├─► MT3608 IN+ ─► OUT 5V ─┬─► L298N  +12V (VS)
                                        │   MT3608 IN- ─► OUT GND ├─► L298N  +5V (logic, jumper OFF)
                                        │                         └─► MG90S  V+ (red)
                                        │
   ALL grounds tie together: 18650- · TP4056 OUT- · TPS63020 GND · MT3608 GND ·
   L298N GND · ESP32 GND · MG90S brown

TP4056

Battery on B+ / B-. Bot draws from OUT+ / OUT-. Charge any time via its USB-C.

TPS63020

Fixed 3.3 V out → powers the brain. Confirm ~3.3 V before connecting.

MT3608

Turn its trim-pot until output reads 5.0 V → powers motors + servo.
⚠ TPS63020 limit
Never feed more than 5.5 V into the TPS63020 — it's a single-cell part. That's why the brain runs straight off the one 18650, not a 2-cell pack.

Remove the ENA and ENB jumpers (so PWM speed control works) and the +5V regulator jumper (we feed 5 V in from the MT3608 instead).

GPIO 38/39/40 are the board's microSD pins — we borrow them for motors, so leave the microSD slot empty for this build.

Pre-power checklist

  • + and – correct at every module (look twice)
  • TPS63020 reads 3.3 V, MT3608 reads 5.0 V
  • All grounds connected together
  • L298N ENA/ENB jumpers removed; +5V reg jumper removed
  • microSD slot empty
  • Wheels off the ground
  • USB unplugged / power switch off

When something doesn't work

Troubleshooting

Upload fails: "Failed to connect… No serial data received"

In order of likelihood:

  1. Wrong port — use the TTL USB-C port, not OTG.
  2. Upload Speed too high — drop to 115200.
  3. Bad cable — must be a data cable, not charge-only.
  4. Button timing — hold BOOT → tap RST → release BOOT → click Upload.
  5. Battery rail on — power switch must be OFF while programming.
  6. Flaky BOOT button — short GPIO0 to GND while tapping RST, then release.
Camera init failed (CAMERA INIT FAILED 0x…)

Almost always the PSRAM setting. Tools ▸ PSRAM ▸ OPI PSRAM, then re-flash. The pin map is identical to your working cameras, so it isn't the pins.

HUD loads but the video box stays black

Video is on port 81. Reload the page (it auto-retries every second). Check the Serial Monitor — the bot still serves the page and drives even if the camera didn't start.

Camera resets every time the motors move

Classic shared-rail noise. The brain must be on the TPS63020 3.3 V rail and the motors on the MT3608 5 V rail, both fed straight from the cell — not from each other. And make sure all grounds are tied together.

The D-pad does nothing

Check the 6 signal wires match the table. Confirm the L298N has +12V (VS), +5V, and GND, and that the motors are on OUT1/2 and OUT3/4.

One wheel spins the wrong way

Swap that motor's two output wires, or flip INVERT_LEFT / INVERT_RIGHT in config.h and re-upload.

Motors run only at full speed regardless of the slider

The ENA/ENB jumpers are still on the L298N. Remove them.

It won't stay upright at rest

Center of mass must be below the axle. Put the battery at the very bottom of the pod and remove anything top-heavy. If it lurches on launch, lower the speed slider.

Phone can't find the "ReconBot" WiFi

Give it ~5 s after power-on; check the Serial Monitor printed AP up. WiFi is 2.4 GHz. Ignore the "no internet" warning — the bot isn't a router.

The educational part

How It Works

Why a "pendulum" instead of a balancing Segway?

Both have two wheels on one axle. The difference is where the weight sits. A self-balancer carries its weight above the axle (like balancing a broom) and needs a computer constantly catching it with a PID controller — hard to tune, especially with cheap gear motors. Our pendulum hangs its weight below the axle, so gravity pulls it upright on its own — no sensor, no control loop. The best fix for a hard control problem is often to redesign so you don't need control at all.

How the motors are controlled — H-bridge + PWM

Flip a motor's voltage and it spins the other way. The L298N has two "H-bridges" — switches arranged in an H around each motor — to flip that voltage from code. For speed, we flick power on and off thousands of times a second; the fraction of "on" time is the duty cycle. That trick is PWM, and it's how nearly everything dims an LED or sets a motor's speed.

How the servo aims the camera

A hobby servo is told an angle, not a speed. It listens for a pulse every 20 ms: a short pulse = one end, a long pulse = the other end. We generate those exact pulses to point the camera up or down.

How video reaches your phone — MJPEG

The camera takes a JPEG photo, sends it, takes another… ~20 times a second. Your eye blends them into motion. It's called MJPEG — not as efficient as "real" video, but dead simple and low-latency, which is exactly what you want when driving a robot.

How the controls reach the bot — a tiny web server

The ESP32 runs a small web server. Port 80 serves the control page and listens for commands like /control?cmd=F; port 81 serves the video so a slow frame never blocks a command. No app store — the bot is a web server and the iPhone's browser is the client.

The failsafe — why the bot stops itself

The page pings the bot every 0.4 s. If the firmware hears nothing for 0.7 s (app closed, out of range), it stops the motors. That's a watchdog — a core safety pattern in robots, cars, and industrial machines.

Why two separate power supplies?

Motors are electrically loud — they dip and spike the voltage. A brain sharing that wire browns out and resets (the #1 beginner headache). So the brain gets its own buck-boost converter that holds a steady 3.3 V even when the battery sags, and the noisy motors live on their own 5 V rail.
